Why this exists

Three out of four veterans with a pending VA disability claim get help from an accredited representative — a county veterans service officer, a VSO, an attorney, or a claims agent. That help is free and it works. But it is spread unevenly across the country, and until now nobody had mapped where it's missing. Where free help is absent, veterans wait longer, give up, or pay private companies thousands of dollars for what a service officer does at no charge.

Federal law now requires VA to prioritize outreach grants to areas with a critical shortage of county and tribal veterans service officers (CVSO Act, §302 of the Dole Act, January 2025). No official shortage map has been published. This site is an independent one, built entirely from public government data and refreshed automatically every week.

Who it helps

County and state officials can see whether their veterans have local help and make the case for funding a service officer — a position that typically returns many times its salary in benefits flowing to local veterans. Veterans service organizations can decide where to recruit and place representatives. Congressional offices get district-level numbers for oversight and for the CVSO Act grant program. Journalists and advocates can show that predatory paid-consultant firms cluster exactly where free help doesn't exist. And veterans can see what's near them — and what should be.

What to know before citing it

"Zero reps" means no representative has a registered address in the county — help may exist nearby or remotely, and accreditation is not the same as active capacity. Every caveat is documented in the methodology. All data and code are open (CC BY / MIT); sources are the VA accreditation roster, VBA workload reports, VetPop projections, VA expenditure data, and Census geography.
